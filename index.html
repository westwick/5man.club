5man.club

Who is this for?

We are a group of players that are tired of playing with random people that don't have any teamwork, don't communicate, and don't care about team comp.

So we've created a framework for how we want to approach the game as well as how we approach each round. This is important to differentiate from "strats".

This framework seeks to minimize common annoyances and pet peeves in Valorant such as:

  • Uncoordinated buy rounds (3 people save, 2 people buy)
  • Competing calls for strats (lack of leader, 2 people trying to both call strats)
  • AFKs (obviously)
  • All 5 teammates rushing the same way on offense (making it really obvious for the other team to rotate and flank)
  • Unclear communications (someone calls "THEYRE ALL COMING A" when its only 3 people)
  • Over communication (talking about non-game stuff, back-seating, etc)
  • Rotating too late or too early, leaving a site wide open before they've committed
  • Team doesn't retake together (goes in 1 by 1...)
  • Pushing too much on defense, playing too passively on offense, peeking when you don't need to
  • Uncoordinated utility usage
  • 4+ duelist comps
  • "Are we going A or B???" - you don't always need to decide at round start, let the round play out a bit first. And don't neglect the middle!

We are creating a system that seeks to minimize or eliminate these annoyances as much as possible. However, we are not trying to "go pro" or play in tournaments. We just want to 5 stack ranked games with the best chances of winning, utilizing team play as much as possible. So read on if you're interested to learn more and see if this system is a good fit for you.

Who is this NOT for?

If you saw this page and your first thought was, "oh man that's too much to read", this team might not be for you. If you just want to jump in games and play, this team isn't for you.

We want to be focused on playing and winning as a team and being as organized as possible both in and out of game.

In other words, a poor plan is better than no plan at all. But I don't think my plan is poor anyway :D and I also think it's important to have one main person driving the framework so we can all work from a unified vision.

So this framework is something I've put together based on all that experience as well as stuff I've just been thinking about over the years. Again, my framework may not be something that C9 or TSM or 100T or whoever can take and win a tournament with, but I hope it's good enough to keep most players diamond and below coordinated.

Organization

Besides the actual framework for how we approach each round, I think it's also important to have everyone on the same page with regards to how we discuss strategy, how and when we play, and basically everything outside of the actual game itself.

As we grow and get more members, I'd like to split the team up by geography and rank, but for now at the beginning, we are focusing on Bronze/Silver/Gold players in US Central and US East. More on this in the future.

Discord is our main place to organize and discuss any changes to the framework, which will be outlined on this site and open for anyone to make suggestions and changes to. The idea is that in your free-time/off-time/whenever we don't have 5 on, you can review the framework online and discuss it with others on discord to become very familiar with it. That way, when we DO have 5 people online, we can maximize our playing time and go immediately into games, since we should already be familiar with the concepts, and not have to waste time reviewing "strats" with someone new or something like that.

The Framework

Ok enough chat, I'm sure you're wondering what the actual framework is at this point. So let's get into it...

The basis of the framework is to split the team up into groups of 2, 2 and 1. Similar to basketball where you have 2 guards, 2 forwards, and a center. This works on both offense and defense and brings several advantages besides just in game tactics.

First let's talk about why organizing the team this way is good from an outside of the game perspective. None of us are pros and we all play for fun, and everyone will have different schedules, and everyone will go through phases where they play a lot and then don't play much at all for a while. We want to ideally have groups of 7-10 people that we can draw from, and it really sucks practicing strats with 4 people and then having a 5th that's clueless. So by breaking the team up into roles, we create a "plug-n-play" system that's easy to rotate players in and out of, and have them instantly know how to work with the team because of the role and the framework.

Another advantage is familiarity. By splitting into two groups of 2, you have a 'partner' you can work with essentially, and when we don't have 5 on, you can just duo queue with your partner and still practice working together, become familiar with each other and know how they like to play, etc. and that will directly translate to our own 5 man games.

Next, as the in game leader, it's easier to reason about 2 groups of 2 rather than 4 individual players. In this system, the 1 person by themself is referred to as the 'lone wolf' and is always the one calling the plays and making the calls mid round, whether they are dead or alive. The other 2 groups of 2 can be assigned any animal name they choose, to make it very easy for the lone wolf to call out strats. As a very simple example, the lonewolf might call out "me and the tigers are going to work mid, bears lurk outside of A and then we will execute together when I say go". Although you still can call more complicated strats, this gives us a nice baseline to work with.

This also gives us a lot of flexibility with how to organize the team each round, i.e. you can have the 2 groups of 2 group up while the lone wolf lurks, to create a 4-1 type of split. Or the lone wolf can group with either team to create a 3/2, or go completely alone from them in a 2-2-1 type of deal. There's lots of options and everyone should be able to easily reason about what their teammates are trying to do. It's also important to note that the groups of 2 don't always have to stick together. They might need to split up for map control, or maybe they are the ones watching the 2 flanks while the other 3 work mid, etc.

The Specifics

So that's the high level overview of how we organize the team in game, but there is a lot more to the framework than just that. This section is a work in progress while I just jot out random thoughts right now. For example, how does it address some of those pet peeves from above?

  • Uncoordinated buy rounds - Lone Wolf will always call whether to buy or save, usually based on if at least 3 people can buy rifles.
  • Competing calls for strats - Lone Wolf always makes the main calls and has the final say
  • AFKs - This shouldn't be an issue on our team, right?
  • Over communication (talking about non-game stuff, back-seating, etc) - Just don't do it on this team :)
  • 4+ duelist comps - Each group of 2 can not have more than 1 duelist. That means, AT MAXIMUM our team will have 3 duelists, and only if the lone wolf chooses a duelist. More often we will probably prefer 2 duelist comps, with a duelist in each group of 2.
